Plants

Welcome to the zoo's plant exhibit, where we showcase some of the most fascinating and beautiful plant species from around the world. Plants are an essential part of the zoo's ecosystem, providing food, shelter, and enrichment for the animals.
One of the most striking plants in our exhibit is the Giant Amazon Lily. This plant is native to the Amazon rainforest and produces large, white flowers that can reach up to a foot in diameter. The Giant Amazon Lily grows in the shallow waters of rivers and lakes, providing shelter and food for aquatic animals such as fish and turtles.
Another fascinating plant in our exhibit is the Venus Flytrap. This carnivorous plant is native to the wetlands of North Carolina and South Carolina and is known for its unique ability to capture and digest insects. The Venus Flytrap has leaves that are modified into trap-like structures that close when triggered by the movement of an insect. The plant then digests the insect for nutrients.
We also have the Titan Arum, also known as the "corpse flower." This plant is native to the rainforests of Sumatra and is famous for its enormous, foul-smelling flower. The Titan Arum can grow up to ten feet tall, and its flower can reach three feet in diameter. The odor of the Titan Arum's flower is similar to that of rotting meat, which attracts carrion beetles and flies for pollination.

Finally, we have the Baobab Tree, also known as the "tree of life." This tree is native to Africa and can live for up to 5,000 years. The Baobab Tree can grow up to 80 feet tall and has a trunk that can reach 40 feet in diameter. The tree produces a fruit that is high in vitamin C and is used for food and medicine by local communities.
In conclusion, plants are an essential part of the zoo's ecosystem, providing food, shelter, and enrichment for the animals. Our plant exhibit showcases some of the most fascinating and beautiful plant species from around the world, and we invite you to explore and learn about the incredible diversity of plant life.

Extinct Plants

Extinct plants refer to plant species that no longer exist in the world today. These plants have gone extinct due to various factors, including habitat destruction, climate change, and overexploitation. The extinction of plant species has significant impacts on the environment and human society, and efforts are being made to prevent further extinctions and conserve endangered species.
One of the most famous extinct plants is the dinosaur-era tree, the Wollemi Pine. This tree was thought to have gone extinct 2 million years ago but was rediscovered in 1994 in a remote canyon in Australia. Today, the Wollemi Pine is one of the most endangered plants in the world, with only a few hundred individuals remaining in the wild.
Another extinct plant is the Hawaiian Hibiscus, which was native to the Hawaiian Islands. The plant was lost due to habitat destruction and the introduction of non-native plant species. Efforts are underway to restore the plant through selective breeding and reintroduction to the wild.
The Franklinia tree, discovered in Georgia in the 1700s, is also extinct today. This tree was last seen in the wild in the early 1800s and is believed to have gone extinct due to habitat destruction and disease. Today, the Franklinia tree is only found in botanical gardens and arboretums around the world.

The Tecopa pupfish plant is another extinct plant species that was native to the United States. This plant was lost due to water pollution and habitat destruction. The plant was the primary food source for the Tecopa pupfish, a critically endangered fish species, and its extinction has had significant impacts on the fish's survival.
In conclusion, extinct plants represent a loss of genetic diversity and the loss of critical ecosystem services. The extinction of plant species has far-reaching impacts on the environment and human society, and conservation efforts are needed to prevent further extinctions and preserve endangered species. The rediscovery of the Wollemi Pine highlights the importance of conservation efforts and provides hope for the survival of other endangered plant species.
